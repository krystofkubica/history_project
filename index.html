Constantinople Anno Domini 1204

The Great City of Constantinople Has Been Sacked!

Word arriving from the East brings shocking news—the great city of Constantinople has been plundered by our holy armies!

Originally called by Pope Innocent III, this Fourth Expedition took a dark turn under the leadership of Enrico Dandolo, the aged and blind Doge of Venice. Instead of marching on the infidels in Palestine, the army utilized ongoing succession disputes as reason to conquer the Byzantine capital itself.

The Pope has declared anathema upon Venice, though he has also sent priests to take over the Byzantine church in an attempt to end the Great Schism.

This impious act of turning our swords against fellow Christians has led to the creation of a new crusader state—the Latin Empire—on Byzantine land, while the Republic of Venice has seized domination over the eastern Mediterranean.

“…the divine body and blood of Christ was spilled upon the ground or thrown about. They snatched the precious reliquaries, thrust into their bosoms the ornaments which these contained, and used the broken remnants for pans and drinking cups,—precursors of AntiChrist…”

— Nicetas Choniates, Byzantine Historian

Jerusalem Anno Domini 1099

The Fall of Jerusalem: We Won!

In the year of our Lord 1099, the Holy City of Jerusalem has finally been retaken by our Christian Knights! The great mission, initiated in 1095 by Pope Urban II, has reached its glorious goal.

“In this temple almost ten thousand were killed… our feet colored to our ankles with the blood of the slain. But what more shall I relate? None of them were left alive; neither women nor children were spared.”

— Fulcher of Chartres, Chronicler

This righteous victory has resulted in the foundation of the Kingdom of Jerusalem, thus successfully establishing Latin dominion over the holiest of lands.

The Rhineland, Marseille & the Mediterranean Anno Domini 1212

The Tragedy of the Innocent

A wave of sorrow has overcome the continent in this year of 1212, as the “Children’s Crusade” ends in complete tragedy. Driven by zeal, a group of youth from the German Rhineland began a long journey on foot, aiming to liberate the Holy Sepulchre. They were strengthened by an innocent belief that the sea would miraculously part before them.

Unfortunately, the youth who reached the French port of Marseille were deceitfully offered free passage by local merchants, only to discover their true fate upon sailing.

“Many thousands of boys, ranging in age from six years to full maturity, left the plows or carts which they were driving… to take the cross. […] Still others got to Marseilles, but whether they crossed to the Holy Land or what their end was is uncertain. One thing is sure: that of the many thousands who rose up, only very few returned.”

— Chronica Regiae Coloniensis

This devastating event has resulted in a massive loss of life and the sale of children into slavery in foreign lands.

Chronicle of the Holy Wars

A Timeline of the Crusades

✠
1095

Declaration of the First Crusade

Actors: Pope Urban II, Peter the Hermit
Cause: Economic and political changes in Europe; the call to “liberate” the Holy Sepulchre.
Result: Launch of the first wave (inexperienced peasants, defeated) and the subsequent second wave (professional soldiers).
1099

Capture of Jerusalem

Actors: Crusader Knights
Cause: Successful military advance of the 2nd wave of professional soldiers.
Result: Establishment of the Kingdom of Jerusalem and 3 vassal states (Edessa, Antioch, Tripoli).
1113

Recognition of the Knights Hospitaller

Actors: The Pope, Knights of St. John
Cause: The necessity to protect pilgrims traveling to the conquered Holy City.
Result: Emergence of a powerful military order (later shifting power to Cyprus, Rhodes, and Malta).
1204

Sack of Constantinople

Actors: Venetians (Enrico Dandolo), Crusaders
Cause: Crusaders’ debt to Venice for the fleet + interference in Byzantine politics.
Result: Foundation of the Latin Empire, weakening of Byzantium, overwhelming commercial dominance of Venice.
1212

The Children’s Crusade

Actors: Children from the Rhineland, fraudulent merchants
Cause: Religious fanaticism and the illusion that only the sinless could liberate the Holy Land.
Result: Absolute tragedy: the children never reached their destination and were sold into slavery.
1291

Fall of the Crusader States

Actors: Christian and Muslim worlds
Cause: Long-term inability to maintain Latin dominion in a completely different cultural environment.
Result: End of the Crusades in the Middle East; the clash of two completely different mindsets comes to an end.

Inheritance Rights as a Strong Motivator

Under the system in effect at this time, only the eldest son could inherit all the land. Younger noblemen were thus excluded from the line of succession. The Crusades therefore presented them with a tremendous opportunity to acquire new land of their own through military conquest and colonization.

Source: Scan, page 74

Two Distinct Phases of the First Expedition

The first wave of the Crusade in 1095 consisted largely of crowds of peasants and the poor who were completely inexperienced in military matters. After moving into Asia Minor, these forces were quickly defeated. It was not until the second wave, composed of trained professional soldiers and knights, that real military successes were achieved and Jerusalem was finally conquered in 1099.

Source: Scan, page 76

The Battle Chant “Deus Vult”

According to the chronicle “Gesta Francorum”, the famous battle chant of the First Crusade was “Deus vult!” (God wills it!). This phrase inspired their zeal and determination to conquer the Holy Land.

Source: museumfacts.co.uk
☩ Connections Across the Ages

The transformative clash of the Crusades deeply overlaps with the study of Arab Society and the Holy Roman Empire, as these holy wars forced a massive exchange of ideas, wealth, and military tactics between the Christian West and the Islamic East. Furthermore, these expeditions profoundly reshaped Medieval Society and fueled the rapid economic rise of Medieval Cities by establishing lucrative Mediterranean trade networks, while simultaneously offering younger knights an escape from the rigid land inheritance laws rooted in traditional Medieval Agriculture.
